The Invisible Wounds

What exactly are the invisible wounds we all carry?

Grief isn't just about death. For military members, veterans, and first responders, grief shows up in ways most programs never address.

💔

Death of a Comrade

The feelings we carry after someone we know has died — in combat, by suicide, or at home.

💔

Divorce & Relationship Loss

The death of a relationship, no matter how long it lasted.

🏆

End of Military Career

Due to separation, retirement, or disability — the loss of identity, purpose, and community.

👫

Estrangement

Broken relationships with family, friends, or others that accumulated during service.

🔒

Loss of Trust

Around leadership, values, or the institutions you served.

😶

Shame Around Service

The things you did, saw, or experienced that you carry in silence.

👤

Transitioning to Civilian Life

The profound adjustment of leaving military life behind.

😢

Survivor's Guilt

The weight of coming home when others did not.

🙏

Comrades Lost to Suicide

The grief compounded by the way they died and the questions that remain.

Common Questions

Questions from Service Members & First Responders

How exactly does the GI Bill cover this training? ▼
Both the Foundational Certification and the Online Capabilities Training are approved for full reimbursement through the GI Bill Chapter 28, 30, or 33 and Vocational Rehabilitation Chapter 31. This means the training can be 100% covered by your education benefits with $0 out of pocket.
Is this therapy or counseling? ▼
No. The Grief Recovery Method is an educational program, not psychotherapy. It's action-based and peer-to-peer. You are not treated as a patient. You are equipped with real tools to deal with grief in a structured, confidential environment. Think of it as a mission — not a diagnosis.
I'm not a counselor. Can I still get certified? ▼
Absolutely. No degree or prior counseling experience is required. Many of our most effective military specialists are veterans, military spouses, chaplains, first responders, and community leaders — people whose own experience with grief ignited a passion to help others.
Will I have to share my personal experiences? ▼
During the 4-day training, you will complete your own grief recovery work as part of the learning process. This is done in a small, confidential group setting. You control what you share — there is no requirement to disclose anything specific. The process is designed to be safe, structured, and respectful.
Can I use this in my VA work or military unit? ▼
Yes. Once certified, you can lead 8-week programs, 2-day intensives, and 1-on-1 sessions (with Advanced certification). Many certified specialists work within VA facilities, military family support programs, veteran service organizations, and first responder peer support teams.
Is this available for first responders too? ▼
Yes. While the GI Bill benefit is specific to military service, the training itself is open to everyone — including law enforcement, firefighters, EMS, 911 dispatchers, and other first responders. Many first responder organizations host private certifications for their teams. Learn about private training →
What makes this different from other veteran grief programs? ▼
Most veteran grief programs focus on coping, support groups, or talk therapy. The Grief Recovery Method is the only evidence-based program (validated by Kent State University) that provides a structured, action-based process to actually complete the grief — not just manage it. It was created by a combat veteran who understood that service members need tools and missions, not diagnoses.

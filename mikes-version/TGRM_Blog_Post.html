Letting Go of Stuff After a Loss: Why It's So Hard and What Really Helps

SM
Sarah Mitchell
February 15, 2024
6 min read
Share:
📷

One of the most emotionally charged tasks after losing someone you love is dealing with their belongings. Whether it's a closet full of clothes, a house full of furniture, or boxes of personal items, the weight of these possessions can feel overwhelming. For many grievers, holding onto these items becomes a way of holding onto the person—but it can also prevent healing and moving forward.

Understanding why we struggle with this process, and learning practical strategies to address it, can transform one of grief's most daunting tasks into an opportunity for growth and closure.

Why We Hold On

When someone dies, their belongings become powerful symbols. Each item carries memories, represents aspects of our relationship with the deceased, and can feel like a tangible connection to them. Letting go of a loved one's belongings can feel like losing them all over again.

Beyond the emotional attachment, many grievers experience guilt or worry about "doing the right thing." Questions swirl: Will I regret getting rid of this? Am I dishonoring their memory? Should I keep everything? This decision paralysis can lead people to hold onto items indefinitely, which often complicates the grieving process rather than supporting it.

Additionally, some belongings carry practical weight. An expensive coat, heirloom jewelry, or valuable antiques create pressure to use, sell, or preserve them "properly." This burden can add to the already overwhelming nature of grief.

"Holding onto possessions isn't about dishonoring the person—it's about finding a new way to honor them while allowing yourself to move forward."

The Action Steps That Help

  1. Start small and give yourself permission to feel. Don't tackle the entire house on day one. Begin with a single drawer or box. Allow emotions to surface—tears, laughter, memories—without judgment. Grief is messy, and that's okay.
  2. Create a "memory keeper" strategy. Instead of keeping everything, intentionally select a few meaningful items. A favorite sweater, a watch, letters, or photos can preserve the connection while honoring the practical reality that you can't keep everything.
  3. Try the photograph method. Before discarding items, photograph them. This captures the memory and frees you from physical storage burden. You can create digital albums organized by category or person.
  4. Donate with intention. Rather than simply discarding items, donate them to organizations that honor the person's values or interests. A musician's instruments to a school, a reader's books to a library, or clothing to organizations helping those in need can feel meaningful and purposeful.
  5. Get support from trusted people. Don't do this alone. Invite a friend, family member, or even a grief counselor to help you sort. Sometimes an objective presence makes decisions easier, and the company provides emotional support.
  6. Set realistic timelines. Grief doesn't have a deadline, but indefinite holding can extend suffering. Work with a grief specialist to establish a compassionate timeline that feels manageable without adding pressure.

Finding Your Way Forward

The goal isn't to rush through this process or to eliminate all connection to your loved one. Rather, it's to transform your relationship with their belongings from a source of burden into something manageable and intentional. The items you keep will mean more because you've consciously chosen them. The memories you preserve digitally remain accessible without taking up physical and emotional space.

Many grievers find that this process, while challenging, becomes part of their healing journey. Each item handled, each memory honored, and each decision made marks progress in integrating the loss into their life narrative. You're not moving on from the person—you're moving forward with their memory in a healthier way.

Remember: there's no "right" timeline or method for dealing with a loved one's belongings. What matters is that your approach honors both your grief and your wellbeing, and allows you to eventually step forward into your life without the weight of unprocessed loss.

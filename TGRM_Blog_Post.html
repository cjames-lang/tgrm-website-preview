The “5 Stages of Grief”: Why They’re Misleading and What Actually Helps

If you’ve ever experienced a loss, someone probably told you about the “five stages of grief” — denial, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance. You may have even felt pressure to move through them in order, like grief has a checklist.

But here’s what most people don’t know: the person who created those stages never intended them for grievers. And after 45+ years of working with people in grief, we can tell you that trying to fit your experience into five neat stages can actually make things worse.

Where the “5 Stages” Actually Came From

In 1969, psychiatrist Elisabeth Kübler-Ross published On Death and Dying, a groundbreaking book about the emotional experiences of terminally ill patients facing their own death. Her observations described how people process the news that they are dying — not how people grieve after someone else has died.

“The stages have evolved since their introduction, and they have been very misunderstood over the past three decades. They were never meant to help tuck messy emotions into neat packages.” — Elisabeth Kübler-Ross & David Kessler, On Grief and Grieving (2005)

Over time, the stages were adopted by pop culture, self-help books, and even clinical settings as a universal framework for grief — despite the fact that Kübler-Ross herself cautioned against this. The result? Millions of grievers have been told their grief should look a certain way, and when it doesn’t, they feel broken.

The Problem With Applying Stages to Grief

When you tell someone that grief follows five stages, three things often happen:

  • They wait. People believe that if they just “get through” the stages, healing will come automatically. They wait for acceptance to arrive like a train on a schedule. It doesn’t.
  • They judge themselves. “I should be past anger by now.” “Why am I back in denial?” The stage model creates a false sense that you’re doing grief wrong if your experience doesn’t match the framework.
  • They miss what actually helps. While waiting for stages to pass, people don’t take the action-based steps that research shows lead to real recovery.

💡 The 40+ Types of Loss Most Frameworks Miss

Grief isn’t just about death. Divorce, job loss, health changes, estrangement, infertility, pet loss, financial loss, and moving are all grief-producing events — and the “stages” model was never designed to address any of them. Find a specialist trained to help with all types of loss.

What the Research Actually Shows

Multiple studies over the past two decades have found that grief doesn’t follow a predictable, linear progression. A landmark 2007 study published in the Journal of the American Medical Association found that while some of Kübler-Ross’s elements appear in grief, they don’t occur in a fixed sequence — and acceptance is often present from the very beginning, not just at the end.

What researchers have found instead:

  • Grief is highly individual — there is no single “normal” path
  • Emotions in grief oscillate and overlap, often within the same day
  • The most effective grief interventions are action-based, not passive
  • Unresolved grief doesn’t go away with time alone — it requires specific emotional work
  • Evidence-based programs, like the Grief Recovery Method (validated by Kent State University), show significantly better outcomes than stage-based models

What Actually Works: Action-Based Grief Recovery

If grief doesn’t follow stages, what does help? After working with hundreds of thousands of grievers over 45+ years, we’ve found that grief recovery requires action, not just understanding. Specifically, it requires addressing the unfinished emotional communications in the relationship affected by the loss.

That might sound abstract, so let’s make it concrete. Think of any significant loss in your life. Now ask yourself: Is there something you wish you had said? Something you wish had been different? Something you wish you could apologize for, thank them for, or forgive? Those unfinished emotional communications are what keep grief stuck — and they’re exactly what the Grief Recovery Method helps you complete.

How the Grief Recovery Method Is Different

The Grief Recovery Method is the only evidence-based grief recovery program in the world, validated by research at Kent State University. Instead of asking grievers to identify which “stage” they’re in, the method provides a structured, action-based framework that helps people:

  1. Identify the specific losses that are causing emotional pain (including the 40+ types most frameworks miss)
  2. Examine the relationship to the loss and the unfinished emotional communications within it
  3. Take action through a guided process to complete those communications
  4. Move forward with new tools and new hope — not by “getting over” the loss, but by recovering from the pain

The process can be done individually with a Certified Grief Recovery Specialist, in small group settings, or through guided self-study. And it works for any type of loss, not just bereavement.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are the 5 stages of grief real?
The 5 stages (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) were originally described by Elisabeth Kübler-Ross in 1969 for people facing their own terminal illness — not for people grieving a loss. While some of these emotions appear in grief, research has not validated them as a universal, sequential model. Grief is far more individual and complex than any stage model suggests.
What actually helps with grief?
Evidence-based approaches like the Grief Recovery Method focus on action-based steps to address unfinished emotional communication, rather than passively waiting for stages to pass. The method is validated by Kent State University research and has helped over one million people recover from grief caused by death, divorce, and 40+ other types of loss.
How long does grief last?
There is no universal timeline for grief. The idea that grief should take a specific amount of time (one year, two years, etc.) is a myth. What matters isn’t the passage of time, but whether you’ve done the emotional work needed to complete your relationship to the pain. Some people begin to feel significant relief within weeks of starting the Grief Recovery Method program.
Do I need a therapist to recover from grief?
Not necessarily. While therapy can be very helpful, the Grief Recovery Method is an educational program (not psychotherapy) that can be facilitated by certified specialists from many backgrounds — including therapists, counselors, hospice workers, coaches, and people whose own grief experience led them to certification. You can find a specialist near you or work online.
